A HARLEM MOTHER

LIFE IS GOOD UNTIL YOU ARE LIVING IT

“By far the most intense contribution was A Harlem Mother, the story of a woman who lost her son to gun violence. Directed by Ivana Todorovic, we are taken through the pain and heartbreak that so many parents living in Harlem have endured and continue to endure today. When the documentary ended and the credits rolled, the theatre was silent – as we struggled with the emotional journey we had just taken”

Susan Peake,
Review Vancouver

In A HARLEM MOTHER, Ivana Todorovic has given us an extremely compelling portrait of activism as portrayed in a singular life. This material will be of excellent use to teachers interested in motivating students toward action as well as investigating how personal stories are effective tools for change. The issues surrounding gun ownership and violence could not be more relevant, giving Todorovic's film significant urgency. 

Annie Howella
Assistant Professor of Film Ohio University

“With clarity, depth and compassion, A Harlem Mother shows the long-term effects of gun violence on a mother's life. Jean Corbett-Parker lost her only son to gun violence years before Ivana Todorovic started making the film, but something that becomes remarkably clear in this story is how the pain of losing a child to gun violence never goes away. This film is an important tool for gun violence prevention advocates, as it has the power to connect broad audiences - teenagers, parents, teachers, legislators, law enforcement - to the issue of gun violence and its ripple effect on society. I would recommend this film to anyone, and have screened it in high schools and community centers throughout New York City. I have seen firsthand how A Harlem Mother enlightens and transforms audiences, often culminating in vigorous discussion. I hope this film continues to be screened all over our country!” Stephanie Skaff ,Director of Beyond Bullets,New York

Synopsis:

In 1998, 18 year old Latron Parker made documentary about difficulties growing up in Harlem. Eights years later he was shot dead on the street. Today his mother Jean fights youth gun violence and helps other parents to survive the pain through her organization, Harlem Mothers. Weaving footage from Latron's own film with scenes from Jean's new life today.
